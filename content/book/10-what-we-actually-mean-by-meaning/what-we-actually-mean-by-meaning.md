# What We Actually Mean by Meaning


When we ask what something means, we usually reach for a definition. A
chair is a piece of furniture designed for sitting, with a seat, a back,
and legs. We can keep going if we want — furniture is equipment,
equipment is made for a purpose, and so on. This way of understanding
meaning works by linking one word or idea to other words and ideas. It
is clear and useful for many things.

But if we stay with this approach, we eventually notice something
strange. The definitions keep pointing to other definitions. We can
describe a chair in more detail, but at some point the words start
circling. They tell us what we already call things, but they don’t fully
explain how those things became meaningful in the first place.

No one learns their first language by looking up words in a dictionary.
Children come to understand what “chair,” “food,” “mother,” or “no”
means through thousands of repeated interactions — being picked up,
being fed, hearing the same tone of voice, seeing the same objects in
the same situations. The meaning arrives through experience long before
the definition does. In many cases, the definition only comes later, as
a way to talk about something that is already understood.

Most of what becomes meaningful to us does not begin with definitions.
It begins when something in the world starts behaving in a reliable way
through repeated contact. A raised surface becomes something you can sit
on. Over time it stops being strange and starts feeling obvious. That
reliability is what we’re calling meaning here — not the label, but the
pattern that has become steady enough to act on without thinking.

Most of the time we don’t think about meaning at all. It’s already
there, quietly doing its work. You wake up in the morning and the bed
feels like your bed. The kettle on the stove is not just metal and water
— it’s the beginning of the day. A message from someone you love carries
a particular weight that a message from anyone else does not. These
things arrive already shaped. We move through them without noticing that
something had to be built, slowly and repeatedly, before they could feel
this way.

If we slow down, we can see that meaning is not one single thing. It
shows up in layers, some obvious and some almost invisible until we pay
attention to how they were made.

Take something as ordinary as a chair.

We can define it easily enough: a piece of furniture with a seat, a
back, and legs, designed for sitting. That definition is clear and
usable. It lets us talk about chairs, distinguish them from stools or
benches, and explain them to someone who has never seen one.

But almost no one actually learns what a chair is by receiving that
definition. Long before we ever hear or read it, we already know how to
use chairs. We know which ones feel stable under us, which ones invite
leaning back, which ones are too deep or too upright. We know this
without thinking. The definition arrives later, if it arrives at all, as
a way of naming something we already know how to do with our bodies.

Even a cat that has never heard the word “chair” can come to know what
one is. After enough time in a room, it learns which raised surfaces are
reliable and which are not. It jumps onto some chairs and not others. It
settles into the ones that are warm in a patch of sun or high enough to
see out a window. It may avoid the one that wobbles or the one that gets
moved constantly. None of this requires language or a definition. The
cat has simply learned, through repeated contact, that certain shapes in
certain places afford certain possibilities.

The same process operates in us. Most of what we know about chairs — how
close to stand when pulling one out, how to lower ourselves into it
without looking, when a chair feels “right” or slightly wrong — was
never taught in words. It was built through thousands of small, repeated
interactions with actual chairs in actual rooms. The official definition
sits on top of that accumulated bodily knowledge. It does not create it.

Even as adults, many of us never fully settle into the standard way of
sitting on a chair. Some people always end up half-crouching, with only
part of their weight on the seat. Others prefer wider or deeper chairs
so they can sit cross-legged. The physical object is the same, but the
relationship with it remains personal. The meaning of the chair
continues to form through how a particular body actually uses it over
time.

This single example already shows us several different ways meaning
appears in real life.

There are things we understand quite well even though we have no clean
name for them. Think of the exact feeling of walking into your own home
after being away for a long time. Your body registers it before your
mind does. The particular quality of light through the windows, the way
the air smells when you first open the door, the sound the floor makes
under your feet, the way your shoulders drop without you deciding to
drop them. All of it arrives at once. If someone asked you to put that
feeling into a single word, you would probably struggle. “Relief” or
“familiarity” point toward it, but they don’t capture the full, specific
pattern your body and memory have built through hundreds of returns. The
understanding lives in the repeated experience more than in any label.

There are also things we have very clear names for, yet the
understanding underneath the name stays surprisingly thin. Many of us
use words like “success,” “respect,” “community,” or “mental health”
without much trouble in conversation. The words feel solid. We can say
them and most people will nod. But if we’re asked to describe, in
concrete terms, what we actually mean when we say them — what daily
patterns they point to, what they require from us day after day — the
answer often turns out to be vague or borrowed from somewhere else. The
name is doing most of the work. The lived substance underneath it is
lighter than it first appears.

Then there are the meanings we share a name for and have some real sense
of, but which still differ from person to person in important ways. Two
people can both say they value “family” and mean something genuine by
it, yet the actual pattern they are maintaining can be quite different.
For one person it might center on regular gatherings and staying
physically close. For another it might center on emotional availability
even when people live far apart. The word travels easily between them.
The underlying pattern each person is maintaining does not. Small
misunderstandings build up because each is treating a slightly different
configuration as “what family means.”

These three layers — understanding without a tidy name, having a name
without much lived substance underneath it, and sharing a name while
maintaining different patterns — cover a surprising amount of our daily
life with meaning. Once we notice them, it becomes easier to see why
some conversations about important things feel slippery even when
everyone is speaking in good faith. We are often not actually talking
about the same pattern.

Meaning is not only found in the things we already have words for. Some
of the most important meanings in our lives are ones we rarely label as
“meaning” at all. They appear when existing patterns interact repeatedly
over time and something new becomes steady enough to carry its own
weight.

Consider what happens when two people decide to build a life together.
At the beginning they are two separate individuals, each with their own
habits, histories, rhythms, and ways of making sense of things. They
like each other. They choose each other. But they are still mostly
themselves, side by side.

Over months and years, through thousands of small repeated interactions,
something additional begins to form. Who usually initiates the
conversation after an argument. How they each show they’re having a hard
day without having to explain it. Who makes coffee in the morning and
who remembers how the other one likes it. How they handle money when one
is anxious and the other is not. How they comfort each other, and how
they celebrate. How they decide what to do with time, and whose needs
get attended to first when there isn’t enough time for everything.

None of these things are negotiated in a single conversation. They are
worked out, often wordlessly, through repetition. Over time, a new
shared reality appears: an “us” that has its own requirements and its
own abilities. It is not simply the sum of the two people.

This new reality can do things neither person could do alone. It can
hold memory that belongs to both lives. It can absorb shocks that would
have been too much for either individual on their own. When one person
is overwhelmed, the other can sometimes carry more for a while, because
there is now a “we” that both are invested in keeping alive. It can
create possibilities for how each person moves through the world that
didn’t exist before.

At the same time, it has new vulnerabilities. It needs ongoing attention
— honest conversation, small rituals of connection, willingness to
adjust — or it begins to weaken. When the way they interact becomes
steady enough, the partnership becomes something that can be counted on.
When it is not tended, the new thing grows thinner and more fragile,
even if both people are still present. You can feel it. Small things
that used to be handled without thinking now require effort.

The same process shows up elsewhere. A team that has worked together for
years develops a way of moving and deciding that no single member fully
controls. Certain risks become visible earlier to the group than they
would to any one person. Certain kinds of trust exist between them that
a new person would need years to earn. People who have been on the team
a long time can sometimes finish each other’s thoughts or anticipate
what someone else will need, not because they are unusually perceptive,
but because the accumulated experience has taught them.

A long-standing weekly ritual — a family meal, a group run, a game night
— can also become its own living thing. It begins to shape the days
around it. People adjust their schedules to protect it. It carries
emotional weight that goes beyond the activity itself. If the ritual is
broken for too long, something feels missing even when nothing obviously
bad has happened.

Even something as personal as a habit can function this way. A morning
walk that began as an effort to get exercise can, after many
repetitions, become something that holds thinking time, emotional
steadiness, and a sense of continuity with oneself. The walk is no
longer just movement. It has become a reliable rhythm the rest of the
day can lean against. When you miss it for several days, you can feel
the difference in how you meet whatever comes next.

In each of these cases, something new has formed through repeated
interaction. This is how much of the meaning in our lives actually grows
— not through big declarations, but through the patient accumulation of
small, repeated interactions that eventually become steady enough to
support new weight and new possibilities.

The same principle explains why real communication can happen even when
language is missing or limited. What matters is not the presence of
words but the presence of aligned or at least compatible patterns
underneath them.

We can often understand a great deal from another person’s tone, timing,
posture, and the way they move through shared space, even when we don’t
share a language. The patterns they are maintaining — what they treat as
important, what they avoid, what soothes them — become visible through
repeated interaction. We don’t need to translate every signal into
words. We need only to notice which configurations are steady for them
and which ones are not.

This is also why communication with animals is possible even though we
cannot have conversations in the human sense. A dog that has lived with
the same person for years has learned a great many patterns in that
person’s behavior. It knows which movements mean food is coming, which
tone means the person is upset, which posture means an invitation to
play. The person, in turn, has learned patterns in the dog — the
particular way it holds its body when it needs to go outside, the sound
it makes when it is content. Neither side has a full verbal language.
Both sides have built enough shared regularity through repeated
interaction that coordination becomes possible.

You see something similar with people who have known each other a very
long time. Two colleagues who have worked side by side for fifteen years
can sometimes make decisions with almost no words. A couple in their
seventies can sit together in comfortable silence and still be in
conversation. The patterns between them have become so well-known that
much of the communication now happens in what is not said.

Meaning, in this sense, is not something added on top of the world after
the facts are established. It is what emerges when patterns become
steady enough to support further recognition and further action. Some of
those patterns receive names and become easy to talk about. Many others
remain mostly wordless yet still shape what is possible for us. Some are
private. Some are shared. Some are still in the middle of forming. All
of them require some form of ongoing attention if they are to remain
strong enough to carry the weight we place on them.

When we begin to notice these layers — the unnamed understandings, the
thin names, the differing meanings under shared words, and the new
realities that appear when patterns interact over time — the ordinary
world starts to look slightly different. A chair is still a chair. A
partnership is still a partnership. But we can also see the patient,
mostly invisible work that went into making each of them usable and
durable. We can see that meaning is not a rare or mysterious substance
reserved for special occasions. It is the quiet architecture that allows
almost everything we rely on to remain standing.

So far we have seen that meaning does not begin with definitions. It
begins when patterns become reliable enough to act on — often long
before we have clean names for them. The Introduction that follows
orients you to the book as a whole and to **The Thread** as a practical
way of working with this architecture consciously. After that, Chapter 1
begins with the oldest human version of the strategy: noticing what
returns, and learning to protect it.

## Key Takeaways

- Definitions link words to other words. They are useful, but they
  circle — and they do not by themselves explain how something first
  became meaningful.
- Meaning arrives through repeated experience long before most
  definitions do. Reliability — a pattern steady enough to act on
  without thinking — is the core of what we mean by meaning here.
- The same object can mean different things depending on bodily
  learning, culture, and personal use. The materials do not change; the
  pattern of interaction does.
- Three common mismatches explain much slippery conversation:
  understanding without a tidy name, a clear name with thin lived
  substance, and a shared name covering different maintained patterns.
- Some of the most important meanings form when existing patterns
  interact over time and become steady enough to carry new weight — a
  partnership, a team, a ritual, a habit.
- Real communication can happen without shared words when patterns
  underneath are aligned or at least compatible.
- Meaning is not added on top of the world after the facts are settled.
  It is what emerges when patterns become steady enough to support
  further recognition and action — and it needs ongoing attention if it
  is to keep carrying the weight we place on it.
